## Discussion Summary

This meeting continued from our first discussion after spending some time exploring the Titanic dataset.

We reviewed the dataset together and agreed that several variables showed clear patterns that would work well for visualization. Passenger class, gender, age, fare, and survival all appeared to have meaningful relationships that could be presented both accurately and, for educational purposes, misleadingly.

One interesting observation was how strongly gender appeared to influence survival. We also noticed that passenger class seemed closely connected with survival rates. These quickly became our leading candidates for the accurate visualizations.

We then brainstormed ideas for misleading charts. Some of the suggestions included truncating the y-axis, filtering out specific groups of passengers, exaggerating differences through scaling, and changing the proportions of chart elements. We agreed that every misleading visualization should still use the original data without changing any values. The only changes would come from the way the data is presented.

At one point we joked that after spending years being told never to manipulate graphs, this assignment was finally encouraging us to do exactly that. We agreed it would actually be a good learning experience because it shows how easy it is to influence someone's interpretation without changing the underlying data.

We also discussed keeping the notebook clean and easy to follow. Rather than writing large blocks of code all at once, we decided to build the analysis step by step with short explanations so that anyone reviewing the notebook could understand our thought process.

### Decisions Made

- Use gender and passenger class as primary visualization topics.
- Create misleading charts through presentation choices instead of modifying data.
- Keep graph titles, labels, and color choices professional.
- Write explanations describing why each visualization is ethical or misleading.
- Continue documenting progress in GitHub throughout the project.
